Chapter Summary: Double Taxation Relief (DTAA)

1. Introduction to Double Taxation

Double Taxation: Taxing the same income twice.

  • Juridical Double Taxation: Same person taxed on same income in two countries (due to Residence Rule & Source Rule). This is addressed by DTAA.
  • Economic Double Taxation: Same income taxed in the hands of two different persons (e.g., company profit tax & shareholder dividend tax). Addressed differently (e.g., MAP).

2. Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements (DTAA)

Agreements (Tax Treaties/Conventions) between countries to prevent/mitigate double taxation.

Objectives:

  • Avoid double taxation.
  • Facilitate Exchange of Information (EoI).
  • Mutual assistance in tax recovery.

Methods of Relief (Commonly):

  • Exemption Method: One country exempts the income.
  • Credit Method: Both countries tax; resident country gives credit for tax paid in source country. India primarily uses the Credit Method.

Power to Enter DTAA: Central Government (Section 90 with foreign countries/territories; Section 90A allows specified associations to enter similar agreements with ratification).

3. DTAA vs. Income Tax Act, 1961

  • If conflict exists, provisions of DTAA or Act, whichever is more beneficial to the assessee, shall apply (Section 90(2)).
  • Exception: A higher tax rate specified in the Act for a foreign company compared to a domestic company is not considered less favourable for the foreign company just because of the rate difference (Proviso to Sec 90(2)). DTAA benefits other than rate still apply.
  • GAAR (General Anti-Avoidance Rule) provisions can override DTAA benefits.

4. Tax Residency Certificate (TRC)

  • Required for a non-resident to claim DTAA benefits in India.
  • Must be obtained from the government of the country of residence.
  • Assessee must also furnish details like Status, Nationality, TIN, Address, PAN (if any) in India (often part of TRC, else separately in Form 10F).

5. Interpretation of Terms in DTAA

  1. If defined in DTAA, use that definition.
  2. If not defined in DTAA but defined in Income Tax Act, use Act's definition (unless context requires otherwise).
  3. If not defined in either, use meaning assigned via government Notification (contextual).

6. Unilateral Relief (Section 91)

Relief provided by India when income is earned from a country with which India has no DTAA.

Conditions:

  • Assessee is Resident in India.
  • Income accrued/arose outside India (and not deemed to accrue/arise in India).
  • Income taxed in both India and the foreign country.
  • Tax paid on such income in the foreign country.
  • No DTAA exists between India and that country.

Calculation of Relief:

  1. Calculate Indian Average Rate of Tax (ART): (Indian Tax / Total Income) * 100. Note: Indian Tax is computed after adding Surcharge & Cess, but before Sec 91 relief.
  2. Calculate Foreign Average Rate of Tax: (Foreign Tax Paid / Foreign Income as per Foreign Law) * 100.
  3. Identify Doubly Taxed Income (Foreign income included in Indian Total Income).
  4. Relief Amount = Doubly Taxed Income * Lower of (Indian ART vs. Foreign Rate)

7. Permanent Establishment (PE)

A concept (often defined in DTAAs, Article 5 in model conventions) determining a country's right to tax business profits of a foreign enterprise.

  • Source country taxes business profits only if the foreign enterprise has a PE there.
  • Includes fixed places like place of management, branch, office, factory, workshop, mine, etc.
  • Distinction: Business *IN* a foreign country (taxable if PE exists) vs. Business *WITH* a foreign country (generally not taxable in source country without PE).
  • A BPO unit can be a PE if it meets the definition criteria.

8. Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) Rules (Rule 128)

Rules governing the mechanism for claiming credit for foreign taxes paid, pursuant to Section 90/90A (DTAA) or Section 91 (Unilateral Relief).

  • Eligibility Year: Credit available in the year the corresponding income is offered to tax in India.
  • Allowed Against: Income Tax, Surcharge, Cess. Not against Interest, Fees, Penalty.
  • Disputed Tax: Claim credit after dispute resolution (within 6 months of resolution).
  • Amount of Credit: Lower of:
    • Tax payable on such income under Indian Income Tax Act.
    • Foreign tax paid on such income.
  • FTC against MAT/AMT: Allowed.

9. Procedural Aspects for FTC (Rule 128)

  • Form 67: Mandatory online form to be filed to claim FTC.
  • Contents: Details of foreign income, tax paid, proof (TDS cert, challan, statement).
  • Due Date for Form 67: On or before the end of the relevant Assessment Year. (Amended from earlier requirement of filing by return due date).
  • Currency Conversion (for Tax Paid Abroad): Convert foreign tax paid to INR using the TTBR (SBI) on the last day of the month preceding the month in which the tax was paid/deducted.
FTC Calculation Clarification (Rate vs. Amount):
  • Generally, compare Indian Avg Rate vs Foreign Rate and multiply by Doubly Taxed Income (as in Sec 91).
  • However, if the actual foreign tax amount (in foreign currency), the date of payment/deduction, and the corresponding TTBR (as per Rule 128) are available, then:
    1. Convert the foreign tax amount to INR using the specified TTBR.
    2. Calculate the Indian tax attributable to that foreign income.
    3. FTC = Lower of these two amounts. (Rate comparison is implicitly done).

10. Currency Conversion for Income Calculation (Rule 115)

Prescribes rates for converting income earned in foreign currency to INR for tax calculation.

  • Salary, Interest on Securities, Dividends, Capital Gains: TTBR (SBI) of the last day of the month preceding the month the income accrues/arises.
  • House Property, PGBP (other than shipping), Other Sources (other than Int/Div): TTBR (SBI) of the last day of the previous year.
  • PGBP - Non-Resident Shipping (Sec 172): TTBR (SBI) of the last day of the month preceding the month income is earned.
  • Exception: If TDS has been deducted in India on any income (Salary, Cap Gains, Int etc.), use the TTBR on the date tax was required to be deducted.

11. Currency Conversion for TDS on Payments (Rule 26)

For deducting tax (TDS) on income payable in foreign currency (e.g., payment to non-resident).

  • Convert the income payable to INR using the TTBR (of that currency) on the date tax is required to be deducted. Calculate TDS on this INR amount.
